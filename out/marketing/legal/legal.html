Priority 1: Do now (before any investor conversations)

IP Assignment Deed

Why it is the single most important document.

Right now, everything you have built — code, designs, brand, product concepts, architecture — is owned by you personally, not by the company. You created it. UK copyright law assigns ownership to the creator. The company is a separate legal entity from you. Without an IP assignment, the company is an empty shell that owns nothing.

Every investor will ask: “does the company own all the IP?” If the answer is no, or “we haven’t done the paperwork,” many will walk away. The IP is what they are investing in. If it is not in the company, they are investing in nothing.

What it covers:

  • All intellectual property you have created prior to incorporation that relates to the company’s business
  • All intellectual property you have created since incorporation
  • Source code, software, algorithms, databases
  • Designs, wireframes, mockups, brand assets
  • Business plans, financial models, market research
  • Domain names, trade marks (registered or unregistered), trade secrets
  • Any work created by contractors or third parties under your direction (you need separate assignments from them)

Key terms:

  • Assignor: you (Richard Targett)
  • Assignee: Formabi Ltd
  • Consideration: typically £1, or “in consideration of the shares already held” — the consideration must exist but can be nominal
  • Warranties: you warrant that you are the sole owner of the IP, that it does not infringe anyone else’s rights, and that there are no encumbrances
  • Future IP: include a clause assigning all future IP created in the course of your work for the company (this overlaps with the service agreement)

When to do it: now. Before any investor conversation. Date it as close to incorporation as possible. This is a 2-page document — not complex, not expensive.

Cost: free if you draft it yourself from a template. £200-500 if a solicitor prepares it.

Founder’s Service Agreement

Why you need a contract with yourself.

A service agreement (or employment contract) between you and the company formalises your relationship. As a sole founder you are both the employer (the company, acting through its director — you) and the employee (you, as a person). This feels circular but it is structurally important:

  1. Investors check it exists. It is a due diligence item. “Does the founder have a service agreement?” Yes or no.
  2. It contains the ongoing IP assignment. The service agreement includes a clause that all IP created during employment belongs to the company. Without it, the IP Assignment Deed covers pre-existing IP but not future work.
  3. It defines your obligations. Full-time commitment, non-compete, non-solicit, confidentiality. These protect the company (and therefore the investor’s money).
  4. It sets salary terms. Even if your salary is £0, it should be documented. When investors come in, they will want to know what the founder draws. Having a formal agreement that says £0 (to be reviewed by the Board) is clean.
  5. It protects you. If investors later want to change your terms, the service agreement is your baseline. Without one, there is nothing to negotiate from.

Key terms:

Term What to include
Role CEO / sole director. Full-time commitment.
Salary £0 per annum (or a nominal amount). “To be reviewed by the Board following any funding round.”
Notice period 3-6 months (mutual). Investors will negotiate this — they want enough time to find a replacement if you leave.
IP assignment All IP created during employment belongs to the Company. Moral rights waived to the extent permitted by law.
Confidentiality Indefinite obligation not to disclose confidential information.
Non-compete 6-12 months post-termination. Must be reasonable in scope (same industry, same geography) or it is unenforceable under English law.
Non-solicit 6-12 months post-termination. Cannot solicit employees or customers of the Company.
Non-dealing 6-12 months post-termination. Cannot deal with customers even if they approach you. Stronger than non-solicit.
Garden leave Company can require you to stay at home during notice period. Rarely used for founders but investors like seeing it.
Termination for Cause Mirrors the “Cause” definition in articles-of-association.md article 38(b).
Expenses Company reimburses reasonable business expenses.
Holiday Statutory minimum 28 days (including bank holidays) or whatever you decide.
Pension Auto-enrolment obligations apply once you start paying yourself a salary above the threshold (currently £10,000/year). Below that, no obligation. At £0 salary, not applicable.

When to do it: now. Before any investor conversation. This is a standard document — 5-10 pages.

Cost: free if you draft from a template. £500-1,000 if a solicitor prepares it.

Cap table

What it is: a spreadsheet showing who owns what, how many shares, at what price, what percentage.

Right now your cap table is simple:

Shareholder Shares Class Price paid %
Richard Targett [number] Ordinary £0.0001 [nominal value] 100%

Check what you actually have: - How many shares were issued on incorporation? - What is the nominal value per share? - Was any share premium paid?

Common setup for UK startups: 100 ordinary shares of £1 each (if using the default Companies House formation) or 10,000 shares of £0.0001 each (if formed via an agent with lower nominal value). Lower nominal value is better — it gives you flexibility to issue small numbers of shares to advisors/employees without meaningful cost.

If you incorporated with £1 shares: consider a share split before your first investment. Subdivide each £1 share into 10,000 shares of £0.0001 each. This gives you 1,000,000 shares (if you started with 100) which is a much more workable number for issuing options and small tranches to investors. This is a simple board resolution + Companies House filing.

What investors want to see in a cap table:

  • Current shareholding (pre-money)
  • Proposed investment: how many new shares at what price per share
  • Post-money shareholding: what percentage each party will hold after the round
  • Option pool: most investors will want a 10-15% option pool set aside for future hires, created pre-money (so it dilutes you, not them)
  • Fully diluted cap table: showing the effect of all options, warrants, and convertible instruments as if exercised

Tools: a spreadsheet is fine at this stage. SeedLegals has a free cap table tool. Carta is overkill for a pre-seed company.

Priority 2: Do before investor conversations start

NDA template

Have a standard mutual NDA ready. You will use it with: - Potential advisors - Potential hires - Commercial partners - Anyone who needs to see confidential product details

You will NOT use it with: - VCs and angel investors (they will not sign — this is industry norm, do not insist) - Accelerators - Anyone you are pitching to publicly

Key terms:

  • Mutual (both parties bound, not one-way)
  • 2-year confidentiality period
  • Carve-outs: publicly known info, independently developed info, legally compelled disclosure, info known before disclosure
  • No obligation to enter into any transaction
  • Governing law: England and Wales
  • No non-compete or non-solicit (keep it clean — an NDA is about information, not restricting business activity)

Length: 2-3 pages. Anything longer signals inexperience.

Cost: free templates available from GOV.UK and various law firm websites. £200-300 if a solicitor prepares a bespoke version.

ICO registration

Legal requirement before you process any personal data. This includes: - A mailing list signup - Analytics on a website - Storing email addresses of anyone (investors, contacts, potential customers) - Using cookies

Cost: £40/year for micro organisations (fewer than 10 staff, turnover under £632k)

How to register: ico.org.uk — online registration, takes 10 minutes

What you need after registration: - A simple privacy policy on your website (see below) - A Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) — even a spreadsheet listing what data you collect, why, the lawful basis, and how long you keep it - Cookie consent on your website if you use analytics or tracking

Privacy Policy and Terms of Service

Required the moment you have any website or product that collects data.

Privacy Policy must include: - Your company name, registered address, and ICO registration number - What personal data you collect - Why you collect it (purpose) - Lawful basis for processing (usually “legitimate interest” or “consent” for a startup) - How long you keep it - Who you share it with (analytics providers, hosting providers, payment processors) - Data subject rights (access, rectification, erasure, portability, objection) - How to complain (to you, and to the ICO)

Terms of Service must include: - What the service is - User obligations and acceptable use - Intellectual property ownership (the product and its content belong to Formabi) - Limitation of liability (cap at fees paid, exclude consequential damages) - Governing law: England and Wales - Termination rights (both sides) - If targeting consumers: comply with Consumer Rights Act 2015 — unfair terms are void regardless of what you write

Priority 3: Build when you are 3-4 months from raising

Business plan

5-10 pages. Not a 50-page MBA document. Investors at pre-seed/seed spend 3-4 minutes reading it. Structure:

  1. Problem (half page) — what pain exists, who feels it, how big the pain is
  2. Solution (half page) — what you are building, how it solves the problem
  3. Market (1 page) — TAM/SAM/SOM, who the customers are, market dynamics
  4. Product (1 page) — what it does, screenshots or wireframes, current state, roadmap
  5. Business model (half page) — how you make money, pricing, unit economics if available
  6. Traction (half page) — what you have done so far. Pre-revenue is fine — talk about product progress, user feedback, pilots, waitlist, LOIs
  7. Team (half page) — you. Why you are the right person to build this. Prior experience, domain expertise, technical capability
  8. Competition (half page) — who else is in the space, why you are different. Be honest — investors know the market
  9. Financials (1 page) — summary of the financial model. Revenue projections, cost base, when you break even, how long the runway lasts
  10. The ask (half page) — how much you are raising, what class of shares, SEIS/EIS qualifying, what the money will be used for

Also needed for SEIS/EIS advance assurance — the business plan you write for investors doubles as the one you submit to HMRC with SEIS1/EIS1.

Financial model

A spreadsheet with 3-year monthly projections. Does not need to be complex. Investors know pre-seed projections are mostly fiction — they are testing whether you understand the economics of your business.

Revenue side: - How you charge (subscription? per-seat? per-form? usage-based?) - Price points - Customer acquisition assumptions (how many new customers per month) - Churn assumptions - Revenue growth trajectory

Cost side: - Your salary (when you start drawing one) - Hires: when, what role, at what salary - Infrastructure: hosting, SaaS tools, domains - Professional services: accountant, solicitor - Marketing: if any - Office: if any (probably not at pre-seed)

Key metrics investors look at: - Monthly burn rate (total costs per month) - Runway (cash in bank / monthly burn) - Months to breakeven - Customer acquisition cost (CAC) — even if estimated - Lifetime value (LTV) — even if estimated - LTV:CAC ratio — should be >3x for a viable business

Format: Google Sheets or Excel. Not a PDF — investors want to play with the assumptions.

Pitch deck

10-15 slides. The visual version of your business plan. Structure follows the same order:

  1. Title slide — company name, strapline, your name
  2. Problem
  3. Solution
  4. Demo / product screenshots
  5. Market size
  6. Business model
  7. Traction / progress
  8. Competition / differentiation
  9. Team
  10. Financials (summary)
  11. The ask — amount, use of funds, SEIS/EIS
  12. Contact

Design: use the Formabi brand colours. Marp or Google Slides. Keep text minimal — the deck supports a conversation, it does not replace one.

Two versions: - Presentation deck — sparse, visual, used when you are presenting live - Reading deck — more text, self-explanatory, used when you email it to an investor who will read it alone

First-Time Founder — What Nobody Tells You

Everything outside the legal checklist. The practical, financial, and psychological things that catch UK founders off guard.

Your personal finances

Before you start spending company money, sort yourself out

The company is a separate legal entity. Its money is not your money. Your money is not its money. Mixing them is the fastest way to create problems with HMRC, investors, and your own sanity.

Personal runway: know exactly how many months you can survive on £0 salary. Not “roughly” — exactly. Total your fixed personal costs (rent/mortgage, bills, food, insurance, debt payments, subscriptions) and divide your savings by that number. That is your personal runway. The company’s runway is a separate calculation.

Benefits cliff: if you were employed, you may lose access to: - Employer pension contributions - Private health insurance - Life insurance / income protection - Death in service benefit - Company sick pay

Consider whether you need to replace any of these personally. Income protection insurance is worth looking at — if you are incapacitated and have no employer sick pay, statutory sick pay is £116.75/week.

Student loans: Plan 1 and Plan 2 student loan repayments are based on income. If your salary is £0, your repayments drop to £0. But if you later take dividends, those do not trigger student loan repayments (dividends are not “earned income” for student loan purposes). This is a legitimate difference, not a loophole.

State pension: National Insurance contributions build your state pension entitlement. You need 35 qualifying years for a full state pension. If your salary is below the NI threshold (currently ~£12,570/year), you are not building NI credits. You can: - Pay voluntary Class 3 NI contributions (~£17/week) to fill gaps - Or accept the gap and make it up later when you start drawing salary

This is not urgent but be aware of it. Check your NI record at gov.uk/check-national-insurance-record.

Directors’ duties — what the law requires of you

As a director of a UK company, the Companies Act 2006 imposes seven statutory duties. You must follow these from day one. Breach of duty can result in personal liability — limited liability protects shareholders, not directors.

The seven duties (sections 171-177)

1. Act within powers (s.171) Exercise powers only for the purposes for which they were conferred. You cannot use company powers for personal benefit.

2. Promote the success of the company (s.172) Act in the way you consider, in good faith, would be most likely to promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members as a whole. You must have regard to: - The likely consequences of any decision in the long term - The interests of the company’s employees - The need to foster business relationships with suppliers, customers, and others - The impact on the community and environment - The desirability of maintaining a reputation for high standards of business conduct - The need to act fairly between members

This is the most important duty. It means every significant decision should be made in the company’s interest, not yours personally. As sole founder and sole shareholder, these interests currently overlap perfectly. They diverge once you have investors.

3. Exercise independent judgement (s.173) Make your own decisions. Do not blindly follow advice without applying your own judgement. You can take advice (and should), but the decision is yours.

4. Exercise reasonable care, skill, and diligence (s.174) The standard is both objective (what a reasonably diligent person with the general knowledge, skill, and experience expected of a director would do) and subjective (what a person with your actual knowledge, skill, and experience would do). The higher standard applies. If you are a technical founder, you are held to a higher standard on technical decisions.

5. Avoid conflicts of interest (s.175) Do not put yourself in a position where your personal interests conflict with the company’s interests. This is why the board minute for your service agreement matters.

6. Not accept benefits from third parties (s.176) Do not accept personal benefits from people dealing with the company (bribes, gifts, hospitality beyond reasonable levels) that could influence your decisions.

7. Declare interest in proposed transactions (s.177) If you have a personal interest in a transaction the company is entering into, declare it to the board. You did this in the board minute for the service agreement.

Practical implications

  • Keep a paper trail of significant decisions (board minutes, even informal ones)
  • When in doubt about a conflict, disclose it
  • Do not use company money for personal expenses. Ever. Not even “I’ll pay it back.” Use a company card for company expenses and a personal card for personal expenses.
  • If the company becomes insolvent or approaches insolvency, your duties shift — you must now consider the interests of creditors, not just shareholders. This is when directors get into trouble. See “wrongful trading” below.

The directors’ loan account — the trap

If you put personal money into the company (e.g. buying a domain with your personal card), the company owes you money. This creates a directors’ loan account (DLA). Conversely, if you take money out of the company for personal use, you owe the company money.

Money you put in (company owes you): this is fine. Keep receipts. The company can repay you at any time without tax consequences.

Money you take out (you owe the company): this is where it gets dangerous.

  • If the loan is over £10,000 at any point in the tax year, HMRC treats it as a benefit in kind — you pay income tax and NI on the value of the benefit, and the company pays Class 1A NI
  • The company must pay section 455 corporation tax (33.75%) on the outstanding loan balance. This is refunded when you repay the loan, but the cash flow impact is real.
  • If the loan is not repaid within 9 months of the end of the accounting period, the s.455 charge kicks in

The rule: do not borrow from your own company. If you need money, pay yourself a salary or dividends through proper channels. If you accidentally put a personal expense on the company card, reimburse the company immediately and record it.

Salary vs dividends — how to pay yourself

When you start paying yourself, you have two mechanisms:

Salary (employment income)

  • Subject to income tax (PAYE) and National Insurance (both employee and employer)
  • Deductible expense for the company (reduces corporation tax)
  • Builds state pension / NI credits
  • Counts as “earned income” for mortgage applications
  • Must run through payroll (PAYE registration with HMRC)

Dividends (shareholder income)

  • Subject to income tax but at lower rates than salary (8.75% basic, 33.75% higher, 39.35% additional — as of 2025/26)
  • No National Insurance
  • Not deductible for the company (paid from post-tax profits)
  • Requires the company to have distributable profits (you cannot pay dividends from an empty or loss-making company)
  • Does not count as earned income for student loan repayments or pension auto-enrolment

The common approach

Most small company directors pay themselves a combination: - A small salary up to the NI primary threshold (~£12,570/year) — this builds pension credits without triggering NI - The rest as dividends — taxed at lower rates than salary

But at pre-revenue with no profits, you cannot pay dividends (no distributable profits to distribute). So the question is moot until the company is making money. Your salary is £0 as documented in the service agreement. When revenue starts, talk to your accountant about the optimal salary/dividend split.

Do not set this up yourself. Get an accountant. The tax implications of getting the split wrong are significant and the rules change annually. An accountant costs £100-200/month for a small company and will save you more than that in tax efficiency.

R&D tax credits

You may be eligible for R&D tax relief even at pre-revenue. If you are developing software that involves technical uncertainty (solving problems that a competent professional in the field could not easily resolve), the qualifying expenditure can generate a tax credit.

For small companies (under 500 employees, under €100m turnover): - You can claim an enhanced deduction on qualifying R&D expenditure - If the company is loss-making (which it is at pre-revenue), you can surrender the loss for a cash credit from HMRC - The credit rate has changed recently — as of April 2024, the merged R&D scheme gives a 20% above-the-line credit for most companies, with an enhanced rate of ~27% for R&D-intensive loss-making SMEs

What qualifies: staff costs (including your notional salary if you set one), software/cloud costs directly related to R&D, subcontractor costs. Your accountant can advise on what counts.

When to claim: with your corporation tax return. You can claim for up to 2 previous accounting periods if you missed earlier claims.

Practical advice: keep a log of what you are building and why it is technically challenging. “Building a form builder” may not qualify. “Building a novel EAV-based datalog architecture for dynamic form schemas that cannot be achieved with standard relational database patterns” probably does. The claim is about technical uncertainty, not commercial novelty.

Get a specialist R&D tax advisor. Most work on a contingency basis (they take a percentage of the claim) so there is no upfront cost. Typical fees: 15-25% of the credit received.

Wrongful trading and your personal exposure

Limited liability means shareholders cannot lose more than their investment. It does NOT mean directors are shielded from everything.

Wrongful trading (s.214 Insolvency Act 1986): if you knew (or ought to have known) that the company had no reasonable prospect of avoiding insolvency, and you did not take every step to minimise losses to creditors, you can be held personally liable for the company’s debts from that point onwards.

What this means practically: if the company is running out of money and cannot pay its bills, you cannot keep trading, running up supplier debts, and hoping things will get better. You must either: - Restructure (cut costs, raise emergency funding) - Seek professional advice (insolvency practitioner) - Cease trading

The moment you know (or should know) the company cannot pay its debts as they fall due, your duties shift from shareholders to creditors. Continuing to trade after this point, hoping for a miracle, is wrongful trading.

Fraudulent trading (s.213) is worse — carrying on business with intent to defraud creditors. This carries criminal liability.

Practical safeguard: monitor cash flow. Know exactly how much runway you have. If you drop below 3 months of runway with no clear path to funding, seek advice. Do not wait until the money runs out.

The emotional stuff

Sole founder isolation

You have no co-founder to talk to. No one who shares the weight. This is the hardest part of being a sole founder and most advice glosses over it.

What helps: - Find a peer group: other founders at the same stage. Not a networking event — a small group of 3-5 people who meet regularly and talk honestly. Accelerators often create these. So do communities like Indie Hackers, Hacker News, or local founder meetups. - Get a mentor or advisor: someone who has done it before. Not for advice on code — for advice on everything else. Most experienced founders will give you an hour a month for free if you ask well. - Separate work and not-work: when you work from home and the company is your baby, there is no natural boundary. Set one. It does not have to be a strict 9-5, but you need time when you are not thinking about the company. - Exercise: not productivity advice. Survival advice. Building a company is physically and mentally stressful. Exercise is the most effective intervention for founder mental health. Make it non-negotiable.

Decision fatigue

As sole founder you make every decision. Technical architecture, legal structure, brand colours, accounting software, which bank account, which domain registrar. Every one. It is exhausting.

What helps: - Make reversible decisions fast. Do not optimise. Pick one and move on. - Make irreversible decisions slowly. Sleep on them. Write down the options and consequences. - Batch administrative decisions. Set aside one morning a week for admin (invoices, filings, accounts). Do not let it leak into every day. - This legal directory you are building is a form of decision-batching. You are making the company structure decisions now, in a concentrated period, so you do not have to make them under pressure later.

Imposter syndrome

You have never done this before. You will feel like you are making it up as you go. You are. Everyone is. The founders who look like they know what they are doing are also making it up — they are just further along and have forgotten how uncertain the beginning felt.

What helps: - Ship something. The fastest cure for imposter syndrome is showing someone your work and having them find it useful. - Talk to customers, not other founders. Founders compare themselves to each other. Customers just want their problem solved.

    1. execute all documents and take all steps reasonably necessary to complete the sale.

  1. If a Dragged Shareholder fails to comply with (3) within 5 Business Days of the proposed completion date, any director is irrevocably appointed as the Dragged Shareholder’s attorney to execute the transfer on their behalf.

  2. The consideration received by each Dragged Shareholder shall reflect the same price per share and form of consideration as that received by the Dragging Shareholders, adjusted only for differences in class rights (including liquidation preferences).

34. Tag-along

  1. If one or more members (the “Selling Shareholders”) propose to transfer in aggregate more than 50% of the issued share capital to a third party (who is not a Permitted Transferee), each other member (a “Tagging Shareholder”) shall have the right, but not the obligation, to require the buyer to purchase a proportionate number of the Tagging Shareholder’s shares on the same terms.

  2. The Selling Shareholders shall give written notice (a “Tag Notice”) to the Company and all other members not less than 20 Business Days before the proposed completion date, containing the same information as a Drag Notice.

  3. Each Tagging Shareholder shall have 15 Business Days from receipt of the Tag Notice to notify the Selling Shareholders in writing that they wish to exercise their tag-along right.

  4. If any Tagging Shareholder exercises their right, the Selling Shareholders shall procure that the buyer purchases the Tagging Shareholder’s shares on the same terms and at the same price per share as the Selling Shareholders’ shares, adjusted only for differences in class rights.

  5. If the buyer refuses to purchase the Tagging Shareholders’ shares, the Selling Shareholders shall not complete the proposed transfer.

Part 8 — Vesting and Leaver Provisions

35. Founder vesting

  1. The Ordinary Shares held by the Founder as at the date of the Company’s first funding round resulting in the issue of Preferred Shares shall be subject to vesting as follows:
    1. 25% shall vest immediately on completion of the funding round (the “Initial Vesting”);
    1. the remaining 75% shall vest in equal monthly instalments over the following 36 months (the “Vesting Period”);
    1. vesting is conditional on the Founder remaining employed by or engaged as a director of the Company.

  1. The Board may, by unanimous resolution, accelerate vesting in whole or in part.

  2. On a Change of Control (being a sale of more than 50% of the issued share capital or a sale of substantially all assets), all unvested shares shall vest immediately.

36. Leaver

A “Leaver” is a person who ceases to be both an employee and a director of the Company, whether voluntarily or involuntarily.

37. Good Leaver

A person is a “Good Leaver” if they become a Leaver as a result of:

    1. death;
    1. permanent incapacity (as certified by a registered medical practitioner);
    1. termination by the Company without Cause;
    1. resignation with the prior written agreement of the Board that the person shall be treated as a Good Leaver; or
    1. the Company undergoing a material breach of the Leaver’s service agreement which is not remedied within 30 Business Days of written notice.

38. Bad Leaver

A person is a “Bad Leaver” if they become a Leaver other than as a Good Leaver, including but not limited to:

    1. voluntary resignation (other than under article 37(d) or 37(e));
    1. termination for Cause. “Cause” means:
      1. a material breach of the person’s service agreement;
      1. commission of an act of fraud, dishonesty, or gross misconduct;
      1. conviction of a criminal offence (other than a road traffic offence not resulting in imprisonment);
      1. a material breach of the person’s fiduciary duties to the Company.

39. Consequences of leaving — unvested shares

  1. On a Leaver ceasing to hold office:
    1. all unvested shares held by the Leaver shall be transferred as follows:
      1. if the Leaver is a Good Leaver: at Fair Market Value (as determined under article 42);
      1. if the Leaver is a Bad Leaver: at the lower of the original subscription price paid and the nominal value of the shares;
    1. the Leaver is deemed to have served a Transfer Notice in respect of all unvested shares at the relevant price, and the provisions of articles 26-29 shall apply as if the Transfer Notice had been given on the date the person became a Leaver.
  1. Vested shares held by a Leaver remain subject to the pre-emption provisions of these Articles but are not subject to compulsory transfer.

40. Fair Market Value

  1. The “Fair Market Value” of a share is the price per share agreed between the Leaver and the Board or, failing agreement within 15 Business Days, the price per share as determined by an independent valuer.

  2. The independent valuer shall be a chartered accountant appointed by agreement between the Leaver and the Board, or failing agreement within 10 Business Days, by the President for the time being of the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales.

  3. The independent valuer shall act as an expert and not as an arbitrator. Their determination shall be final and binding (absent manifest error).

  4. The costs of the independent valuer shall be borne equally by the Company and the Leaver.

Part 9 — General Meetings and Resolutions

41. Calling general meetings

  1. The directors may call a general meeting at any time.

  2. The directors must call a general meeting if required to do so by the members under section 303 of the Act.

  3. General meetings shall be called by not less than 14 clear days’ notice (or such shorter period as the Act permits with the agreement of the requisite majority).

42. Quorum for general meetings

  1. No business other than the appointment of the chair of the meeting is to be transacted at a general meeting if the persons attending it do not constitute a quorum.

  2. A quorum shall be one qualifying person present in person or by proxy, provided that:

    1. after the issue of Preferred Shares, the quorum shall include at least one Investor Shareholder (or their proxy); and
    1. if the Company has more than one member, the quorum shall be at least two qualifying persons.

43. Attendance and speaking at general meetings

  1. A person is able to exercise the right to speak at a general meeting when that person is in a position to communicate to all those attending the meeting, during the meeting, any information or opinions which that person has on the business of the meeting.

  2. A person is able to exercise the right to vote at a general meeting when they are able to vote and have their vote counted at the same time as the votes of all other persons attending the meeting.

  3. The directors may make whatever arrangements they consider appropriate to enable those attending a general meeting to exercise their rights, including participation by electronic means.

44. Votes of members

  1. On a vote on a resolution on a show of hands, each member present in person or by proxy shall have one vote.

  2. On a vote on a resolution on a poll, every member shall have one vote for each share held.

45. Proxies

  1. A member is entitled to appoint another person as their proxy to exercise all or any of their rights to attend, speak, and vote at a general meeting.

  2. Proxies may only validly be appointed by a notice in writing (a “proxy notice”) which states the name and address of the member, identifies the proxy, and is signed by or on behalf of the member.

46. Written resolutions

  1. A resolution in writing agreed by members holding the requisite majority shall be as effective as if passed at a general meeting.

  2. A written ordinary resolution requires agreement by members holding more than 50% of the total voting rights.

  3. A written special resolution requires agreement by members holding 75% or more of the total voting rights.

Part 10 — Dividends and Distributions

47. Dividends

  1. The Company may by ordinary resolution declare dividends, and the directors may decide to pay interim dividends.

  2. No dividend may be declared or paid unless it is in accordance with members’ respective rights and the directors’ recommendation.

  3. No dividend may be declared or paid unless the directors have made a recommendation as to its amount. Such a dividend may not exceed the amount recommended by the directors.

  4. No dividend may be declared or paid unless the Company has sufficient distributable profits.

  5. Unless the rights of a class of shares provide otherwise, dividends shall be declared and paid on Ordinary Shares and Preferred Shares in the order specified by the terms of issue of the Preferred Shares.

48. Payment of dividends

  1. Where a dividend is to be paid in cash, it must be paid by bank transfer to the account specified by the member.

  2. The Company may deduct from any dividend payable to a member any sums presently payable by the member to the Company.

Part 11 — Winding Up and Liquidation Preference

49. Distribution on winding up

  1. On a return of capital on winding up or otherwise (other than a conversion, redemption, or purchase of shares), the assets available for distribution shall be applied in the following order:
    1. First, in paying to the holders of Preferred Shares an amount equal to the higher of:
      1. the aggregate subscription price paid for those Preferred Shares, plus any declared but unpaid dividends (“Liquidation Preference”); or
      1. the amount those holders would receive if the Preferred Shares had been converted to Ordinary Shares immediately before the return of capital;
    1. Second, the balance (if any) shall be distributed among the holders of Ordinary Shares pro rata to their holdings.
  1. This article is subject to the terms of issue of any Preferred Shares, which may specify a participating or non-participating liquidation preference or a multiple of the subscription price.

Part 12 — Anti-dilution

50. Anti-dilution protection

  1. If the Company issues shares at a price per share less than the price paid by the holders of Preferred Shares in the most recent funding round (a “Down Round”), the holders of Preferred Shares shall be entitled to anti-dilution protection on a broad-based weighted average basis, unless the terms of issue specify otherwise.

  2. Anti-dilution protection shall be implemented by adjusting the conversion ratio at which Preferred Shares convert to Ordinary Shares, rather than by the issue of additional shares.

  3. The following issuances are excluded from anti-dilution adjustments:

    1. shares issued under an employee share option scheme approved by the Board;
    1. shares issued on conversion of convertible loan notes;
    1. shares issued as consideration for the acquisition of another company or business;
    1. shares issued with the consent of the Investor Majority.

Part 13 — Information Rights

51. Information rights

  1. Each Investor Shareholder (or group of Investor Shareholders acting together) holding at least 5% of the Company’s issued share capital shall be entitled to receive:
    1. unaudited monthly management accounts within 30 days of month end;
    1. annual audited accounts (once the Company is required to prepare them);
    1. an annual budget and business plan;
    1. prompt notification of any event that is material to the Company’s business, financial condition, or prospects.

  1. The Company shall maintain proper books and records in accordance with the Act.

  2. Information provided under this article shall be subject to the confidentiality obligations of any shareholders’ agreement then in force.

Part 14 — Conversion Rights

52. Conversion of Preferred Shares

  1. Each holder of Preferred Shares may convert some or all of their Preferred Shares into Ordinary Shares at any time by giving written notice to the Company.

  2. The initial conversion ratio shall be 1:1 (one Preferred Share converts to one Ordinary Share), subject to adjustment for:

    1. any anti-dilution adjustment under article 50;
    1. any subdivision, consolidation, or reorganisation of the share capital.
  1. All Preferred Shares shall automatically convert to Ordinary Shares on:
    1. an IPO; or
    1. the written election of the Investor Majority.

Part 15 — Miscellaneous

53. Indemnity and insurance

  1. Subject to section 232 of the Act, the Company may indemnify any director or former director against any liability incurred in connection with any negligence, default, breach of duty, or breach of trust in relation to the Company, to the extent permitted by sections 232-235 of the Act.

  2. The directors may purchase and maintain insurance, at the expense of the Company, for the benefit of any director or former director against any liability incurred in connection with their duties as a director.

54. Means of communication

  1. Any notice or document may be sent to a member by electronic means to the address the member has provided for that purpose.

  2. A member who has not provided an electronic address shall be entitled to receive notices and documents by post.

55. Company secretary

The directors may appoint and remove a company secretary, and shall do so if required by the Act.

56. Seal

If the Company has a seal, it shall only be used by the authority of the directors or of a committee authorised by the directors.

57. Governing law

These Articles shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of England and Wales. The courts of England and Wales shall have exclusive jurisdiction in relation to any dispute arising out of or in connection with these Articles.

Articles of Association — Founder’s Guide

Companion to articles-of-association.md. Read this first. The articles are the template; this is how to think about them.

When to adopt these articles

Not yet. Keep the Model Articles until you have a reason to switch.

Why Model Articles are fine for now

Model Articles (Companies Act 2006, Schedule 1) are thin — but that’s the point. With one shareholder there is nobody to protect against, nobody to grant board seats to, nobody who needs pre-emption rights. The bespoke articles are machinery for managing multiple shareholders. Running that machinery with one shareholder is overhead with no benefit.

What Model Articles give you as a sole founder:

  • Companies House recognises them without question
  • No legal review needed (they are statutory defaults)
  • Full control — sole director, sole shareholder, no reserved matters blocking you
  • Any resolution is passed by you signing it
  • You can act fast — no notice periods to yourself

What the bespoke articles do that you do not need yet:

Clause Why it is irrelevant today
Pre-emption rights You cannot pre-empt yourself
Drag/tag-along One shareholder cannot drag or tag themselves
Board composition You are the board
Investor director appointments No investors
Reserved matters You would be consenting to yourself
Leaver provisions You cannot leave yourself

The trigger: the moment you are about to have a second shareholder

There are three scenarios that trigger adoption:

1. Equity investment round (most common)

The bespoke articles are adopted as part of the funding documents. The investor’s lawyer will negotiate the terms. Your articles become part of a package alongside the subscription agreement, shareholders’ agreement, and disclosure letter.

Adopting articles in advance and then having an investor’s lawyer redline them is wasted work — and wasted legal spend. Every investor’s counsel will want changes. The template here is your negotiation starting position, not a final document.

2. Issuing shares to a co-founder or early employee

If you give someone equity (not options), you want pre-emption, vesting, and leaver provisions in place before the shares are issued. Once someone holds shares with no transfer restrictions, you have a structural risk — under Model Articles a shareholder can theoretically transfer shares to anyone without your consent.

3. Setting up an EMI option scheme

You can technically run EMI under Model Articles, but most advisors recommend bespoke articles at the same time because EMI exercise creates new shareholders.

The adoption process

When the time comes:

  1. Special resolution — pass a written special resolution to adopt new articles in substitution for the existing Model Articles. Since you hold 100% of voting rights, you pass it alone.
  2. File at Companies House — Form CC04 (change of articles) with the full text of the new articles attached. Filing fee is negligible.
  3. Timing — file the special resolution and new articles at the same time as (or immediately before) the share allotment that creates your new shareholders.

The entire thing can be done in a day. There is no advantage to doing it months in advance.

How to use the template now

The template is your negotiation starting position. When an investor comes to the table, you share this and say “here are our proposed articles.” Their lawyer redlines it. Knowing what every clause does — and where the negotiation pressure points are — means you are not starting from zero.

Common investor negotiation points

These are the clauses investors push hardest on. Know your position before they arrive.

Drag-along threshold (article 33)

The template says 75%. Investors may push for a simple majority (50%+1).

  • 75% protects you as founder — an investor with 30% cannot drag you into a sale you disagree with
  • 50%+1 gives investors more flexibility to force a sale, especially if they hold a significant minority
  • Typical outcome: 75% at seed, may drop to 66.7% or lower at Series A+ where investors collectively hold more

Your position: hold at 75% for as long as possible. This is one of your strongest protections.

Liquidation preference (article 49)

The template drafts a 1x non-participating preference — the investor gets back their money OR converts to ordinary shares and takes pro rata, whichever is higher. This is founder-friendly.

Investors may push for:

  • 1x participating (“double dip”) — they get their money back AND then share in the remaining proceeds pro rata. This significantly reduces what ordinary shareholders receive on a modest exit.
  • Multiple (2x, 3x) — they get 2x or 3x their investment back before ordinary shareholders see anything. Rare at seed, more common in later rounds or down rounds.

Your position: 1x non-participating is market standard for UK seed. Resist participating unless you have no leverage. If you must accept participating, push for a cap (e.g. 3x return, after which it converts).

Anti-dilution (article 50)

The template uses broad-based weighted average. This is the founder-friendly option.

Investors may push for:

  • Full ratchet — if a down round happens, the investor’s conversion price drops to the new lower price as if they had invested at that price. This is punitive to founders and massively dilutive.
  • Narrow-based weighted average — less dilutive than full ratchet but more dilutive than broad-based, because it uses a smaller denominator in the calculation.

Your position: broad-based weighted average is market standard in the UK. Full ratchet is a red flag — it signals an investor who expects the company to struggle.

Reserved matter thresholds (Schedule 1)

The template sets thresholds at:

  • Borrowing/capex: £25,000
  • Contracts: £50,000/year or £100,000 aggregate
  • Director/employee pay changes: £75,000/year

These are set at typical pre-seed/seed levels. They should scale with the company:

Stage Borrowing/capex Contracts Pay threshold
Pre-seed/seed £25k £50k/£100k £75k
Series A £100k £200k/£500k £120k
Series B+ £250k+ £500k+/£1m+ Negotiate

Your position: make sure the thresholds are workable for day-to-day operations. Too low and you need investor consent for routine spending. Too high and the protections are meaningless. The key test: “will I hit this threshold in normal operations within 6 months?” If yes, it is too low.

Board seats (article 9)

The template gives investors:

  • One board seat on first funding round
  • A second seat if they take 25%+ of issued share capital
  • The founder stays as director while holding ≥10%

Investors may push for:

  • A board seat at a lower threshold
  • Board majority (two investor directors vs one founder director) — resist this strenuously
  • Chair to rotate to an investor director

Your position: the founder should maintain board control at seed. A common structure is 2:1 (two founder-side directors, one investor director) or 2:1:1 (two founder, one investor, one independent). Never give up board majority at seed.

Vesting (article 35)

The template uses 25% immediate + 36-month monthly vesting, triggered at the first funding round. This is favourable to the founder because:

  • No vesting applies while you are the sole shareholder (pre-investment work is not at risk)
  • 25% immediate means you keep a quarter even if things go wrong immediately after funding
  • Monthly vesting (not quarterly or annual cliff) means smooth accumulation

Investors may push for:

  • A 12-month cliff before any vesting begins (standard in the US, less common in UK seed)
  • A longer vesting period (48 months instead of 36)
  • Reverse vesting on all shares from day one, not just from funding

Your position: the template position is good. The key argument is that you have been building before investment — the 25% immediate vest recognises pre-investment work. Push back hard on any attempt to reverse-vest shares you held before funding.

Clause-by-clause reference

What each part of the articles does, in plain language.

Part 1 — Interpretation (articles 1-3)

Definitions. The £0.0001 nominal value per ordinary share is standard for UK startups — it means you can issue 10,000 shares for £1, keeping the share price low and flexible for option schemes.

Part 2 — Directors (articles 4-7)

Standard director powers. Nothing unusual. Article 6 lets you delegate to committees — useful later for audit committee, remuneration committee, etc. Not needed at seed.

Part 3 — Board composition (articles 8-18)

This is where control lives.

  • Article 8(2): you can be sole director pre-investment. After investment, minimum two directors in practice (you + investor director).
  • Article 9(4): your right to remain a director while holding ≥10% of ordinary shares. This is your safety net — even after multiple dilutive rounds, you stay on the board until you drop below 10%.
  • Article 10(2): investors cannot remove you as director under s.168 Companies Act while you hold ≥10%. This blocks the “fire the founder” scenario.
  • Article 15: you are Chair. The Chair runs meetings and sets the agenda — this is soft power but real power.
  • Article 16: your casting vote disappears when preferred shares are issued. This is standard — investors will not accept a structure where you can unilaterally break ties.
  • Article 18: observer rights. A compromise between a board seat and nothing. Investors who do not get a board seat often get an observer seat instead.

Part 4 — Reserved matters (articles 19-20)

The investor’s veto list. After funding, you cannot do any of the 23 things in Schedule 1 without investor consent. This is normal and expected. The negotiation is about the thresholds, not the principle.

Article 20 (deadlock) matters when the board cannot agree. The escalation path is: 30 days deadlock → refer to appointers → 20 days good faith negotiation → CEDR mediation. This avoids litigation as a first resort.

Part 5 — Share capital (articles 21-24)

Framework for issuing different classes of shares. Article 21(2) lists the typical rights that preferred shares carry — but the actual terms are set in the subscription agreement for each round, not hardcoded here. This gives flexibility for different rounds to have different terms.

Part 6 — Pre-emption on transfer (articles 25-32)

The core transfer restriction. No shareholder can sell to an outsider without first offering to existing shareholders. The timeline:

Seller gives Transfer Notice
    ↓ 10 Business Days
Company sends Offer Notice to all other members
    ↓ 20 Business Days (Acceptance Period)
Members accept/decline
    ↓ 10 Business Days
Completion (if all taken up)
    OR
    ↓ 60 Business Days
Seller may sell to outsider at same or higher price

Total timeline: worst case ~100 Business Days (~5 months) from notice to external sale. This is slow by design — it gives existing shareholders time to act.

Permitted transfers (article 30): transfers to family, trusts, or nominees bypass pre-emption. This lets you do estate planning without triggering the full process.

Death (article 32): your personal representatives can either register as shareholders or transfer — but transfers are still subject to pre-emption. This protects the company from unwanted shareholders while respecting the estate.

Part 7 — Drag and tag (articles 33-34)

Drag-along: if 75%+ of shareholders want to sell the entire company, they can force the remaining shareholders to sell too, on the same terms. The power of attorney in article 33(4) means a holdout shareholder cannot block the deal by refusing to sign — a director signs for them. This is essential for a clean exit.

Tag-along: if someone tries to sell 50%+ of the company, minority shareholders can tag along and sell their shares too, on the same terms. This protects minorities from a change of control where the new majority has no obligation to them.

The interplay: drag protects the majority from holdouts. Tag protects the minority from being left behind. Both use the same price per share, adjusted for class rights (so preferred shareholders get their liquidation preference before ordinary shareholders calculate their per-share amount).

Part 8 — Vesting and leavers (articles 35-40)

Vesting (article 35): only kicks in at first funding. Pre-investment, you own 100% outright with no restrictions. Post-investment, 25% vests immediately, 75% over 36 months. Change of control accelerates everything — if the company is sold, all your shares vest so you get full value.

Good Leaver (article 37): death, incapacity, fired without cause, constructive dismissal, or resignation with board consent. Good Leavers get Fair Market Value for unvested shares — they are not punished.

Bad Leaver (article 38): voluntary resignation or fired for cause (fraud, gross misconduct, criminal conviction, breach of fiduciary duty). Bad Leavers get nominal value — effectively forfeit their unvested shares.

Fair Market Value (article 40): agreed between parties, or determined by an independent chartered accountant. The ICAEW President appoints if the parties cannot agree on a valuer. This avoids litigation.

The leaver provisions apply to anyone with shares subject to vesting — not just the founder. When you issue shares to employees or co-founders later, the same mechanism applies.

Part 9 — General meetings (articles 41-46)

Standard Companies Act procedure. The key investor protection is article 42(2)(a) — after funding, the quorum for general meetings includes at least one investor shareholder. This means you cannot pass shareholder resolutions without investor presence.

Part 10 — Dividends (articles 47-48)

Standard. Dividends require board recommendation and ordinary resolution. The practical effect: you cannot extract cash via dividends without investor consent (because it is a reserved matter in Schedule 1, item 4).

Part 11 — Liquidation preference (article 49)

On winding up or sale, the investor gets the higher of:

  • Their money back (1x subscription price + unpaid dividends), OR
  • What they would get if they converted to ordinary shares and took pro rata

This is 1x non-participating. The investor never loses money on a total loss scenario (they get priority), but on a large exit they convert and share proportionally with ordinary shareholders.

Example — investor puts in £500k for 20% of the company:

Exit value Investor gets (non-participating) Founder gets
£250k £250k (all of it — liquidation preference capped at available assets) £0
£500k £500k (1x preference) £0
£1m £500k (preference) or £200k (20% pro rata) → takes £500k £500k
£5m £500k (preference) or £1m (20% pro rata) → converts, takes £1m £4m
£50m Converts, takes £10m (20%) £40m

If this were 1x participating, the investor would take their £500k back AND 20% of the remainder. On a £5m exit: £500k + 20% of £4.5m = £1.4m instead of £1m. The difference grows with exit size.

Part 12 — Anti-dilution (article 50)

Protects investors if you raise at a lower valuation later (a “down round”). Broad-based weighted average adjusts the conversion ratio — the investor’s preferred shares convert into more ordinary shares, partially compensating for the lower price.

The exclusions in article 50(3) are important — EMI options, convertible notes, and acquisitions do not trigger anti-dilution. Without these carve-outs, routine share issuances would trigger adjustments.

Part 13 — Information rights (article 51)

Investors with 5%+ get monthly management accounts within 30 days of month end. This is a real operational commitment — you need to produce management accounts monthly. Start this discipline now, even without investors, so it is not a scramble later.

Part 14 — Conversion (article 52)

Preferred shares convert to ordinary shares 1:1 (adjusted for anti-dilution and reorganisations). Automatic conversion on IPO or investor election. This is standard — at IPO, all shares become ordinary to simplify the listing.

Part 15 — Miscellaneous (articles 53-57)

D&O indemnity and insurance (article 53) — get D&O insurance when you have a board. Budget £1,500-3,000/year at seed stage.

Everything governed by English law (article 57). Non-negotiable for a company incorporated in England.

Schedule 1 — Reserved matters

23 items. The investor’s checklist of things you cannot do without asking. Read every one and test it against your operations: “would I hit this in normal business?” If yes, negotiate the threshold up.

The items that bite most often at seed stage:

  • Item 9 (borrowing >£25k): if you plan to use invoice financing, R&D tax credit loans, or any debt facility, £25k may be too low
  • Item 11 (capex >£25k): a single server purchase or office fit-out could hit this
  • Item 18 (pay changes >£75k): if your first engineering hire will earn more than £75k, every salary review needs investor consent
  • Item 19 (contracts >£50k/year): AWS, Google Cloud, or a significant SaaS contract could hit this

Schedule 2 — Deed of adherence

Template for new shareholders to sign. Anyone who receives shares (by transfer or allotment) signs this to confirm they are bound by the articles. Without it, a new shareholder could argue they are not party to the restrictions.

  1. Read the template — understand every clause so you are not learning during a funding negotiation
  2. Keep Model Articles — do not file the bespoke articles at Companies House yet
  3. Get a solicitor relationship — you do not need them on retainer, but have someone identified. Recommendations for UK startup solicitors:
    • SeedLegals (automated, cheaper, good for seed)
    • Orrick (top-tier VC firm, expensive but thorough)
    • Taylor Wessing (strong UK startup practice)
    • Shoosmiths (good mid-market option)
    • Keystone Law (flexible, consultant model, often cheaper)
  4. Budget £1,500-3,000 for articles review when the time comes. This is the cost of a solicitor reviewing and customising the template for your specific situation.
  5. Start producing monthly management accounts now — even if it is just a spreadsheet. When investors arrive and article 51 kicks in, you need this to be routine, not a scramble.

The one exception: adopt early if you issue shares to anyone

If you issue shares to a co-founder, advisor, or employee before a funding round, adopt the bespoke articles first. Model Articles have almost no transfer restrictions — a shareholder could theoretically transfer shares to anyone without your consent. The pre-emption rights in articles 25-32 close this gap.

The sequence:

  1. Pass special resolution to adopt bespoke articles
  2. File Form CC04 at Companies House
  3. Then allot the new shares

Never the other way around.

SEIS/EIS — Founder’s Guide

Tax relief schemes that make angel investment in your company dramatically cheaper for investors. This is about your investors’ tax, not yours.

What these schemes are

SEIS (Seed Enterprise Investment Scheme) and EIS (Enterprise Investment Scheme) are UK government programmes that give individual investors tax relief when they invest in qualifying early-stage companies. They are administered by HMRC.

The schemes exist because early-stage investing is risky. The government subsidises the risk by giving the investor tax breaks, which makes the effective cost of the investment much lower. For founders, this means UK angels are far more likely to invest — and many will not invest at all without SEIS/EIS qualification.

What the investor gets

SEIS (the better one — use this first)

Relief Detail
Income tax relief 50% of the amount invested, deducted from the investor’s income tax bill
Capital gains tax exemption If the investor holds for 3+ years, any gain on selling the shares is completely tax-free
Capital gains reinvestment relief If the investor reinvests a capital gain into SEIS shares, 50% of the reinvested gain is exempt from CGT
Loss relief If the company fails, the investor can offset their loss (net of income tax relief already received) against income tax or CGT

Example: an investor puts £50,000 into your SEIS round.

  • They get £25,000 back as income tax relief (50%)
  • Their effective cost is £25,000
  • If the company succeeds and they sell after 3 years, the gain is tax-free
  • If the company fails completely, they lose £25,000 (not £50,000) and can offset that loss against other income at their marginal rate — if they pay 45% income tax, the loss relief is worth £11,250, so total loss is £13,750 on a £50,000 investment

The downside protection is extraordinary. An investor paying 45% income tax who invests £50,000 through SEIS risks only £13,750 if the company goes to zero. That is a 72.5% effective subsidy.

EIS (still good — use after SEIS is exhausted)

Relief Detail
Income tax relief 30% of the amount invested
Capital gains tax exemption If held for 3+ years, any gain is tax-free
Capital gains deferral Investor can defer a capital gain by reinvesting into EIS shares (the gain is deferred, not exempted)
Loss relief Same as SEIS — loss net of income tax relief can be offset against income

EIS is less generous than SEIS but has much higher limits (£12m lifetime vs £350k).

Why this matters for fundraising

Most UK angel investors expect SEIS/EIS. When you pitch, the conversation is not “do you offer SEIS?” — it is assumed. Not having SEIS/EIS qualification is a deal-breaker for many angels because:

  1. The effective cost of their investment is 50-72.5% lower with SEIS
  2. The downside protection transforms the risk profile
  3. Many angel networks and syndicates have SEIS/EIS as a mandatory filter
  4. Fund managers investing via EIS funds can only deploy into EIS-qualifying companies

Getting advance assurance is not a nice-to-have. It is a prerequisite for raising angel money in the UK.

The trading clock — why pre-trading is the best position

The 2-year SEIS age limit runs from the date the company begins to trade, not from incorporation. “Trading” means carrying on a commercial activity with a view to profit. HMRC’s definition:

Activity Is this “trading”?
Writing code / building the product No — preparing to trade
Hiring employees to build the product No — preparing to trade
Buying equipment, servers, domains No — preparing to trade
Market research, customer discovery No — preparing to trade
Setting up the company, opening bank accounts No — preparing to trade
Building a website to attract future customers No — preparing to trade
Running a free beta / pilot with no payment Grey area — likely no, but take advice
Invoicing your first paying customer Yes — trading has begun
Signing a paid contract for services Yes — trading has begun
Receiving payment for the product Yes — trading has begun

Formabi is pre-trading. The SEIS 2-year clock has not started. This means:

  1. You can take as long as you need building the product before the clock starts
  2. You can raise SEIS money while pre-trading (the scheme explicitly allows companies “preparing to carry on a qualifying trade”)
  3. The 2-year window only begins when you make your first commercial sale
  4. You could theoretically incorporate, build for 3 years, start trading, and still have 2 years of SEIS eligibility from that point

This is the strongest possible position for SEIS. You have maximum flexibility on timing. There is no urgency driven by the clock — only the urgency of when you actually need the money.

What to watch for: the moment you charge anyone for anything — even a small pilot fee — the clock starts. If you want to run paid pilots while preserving SEIS eligibility, plan the timing. Once the first invoice goes out, you have 2 years.

The process — step by step

Step 1: Check qualification (do now)

Go through the criteria tables above. For Formabi the likely issues are:

  • Age for SEIS: the 2-year clock starts from when the company began trading, not incorporation. “Trading” means carrying on commercial activity with a view to profit. If the company was incorporated in June 2024 and began trading immediately, the SEIS window closes around June 2026. If it began trading later (e.g. building product before first revenue), the window may be longer. Get a definitive date.
  • Gross assets for SEIS: must be under £350k immediately before the share issue. If you have raised any money, bought any equipment, or hold any IP on the balance sheet, check the total.

Step 2: SEIS Advance Assurance (SEIS1 form) — do before raising

Submit HMRC form SEIS1. This is an application asking HMRC to confirm in advance that your company qualifies for SEIS. It is not mandatory — you can issue shares and apply afterwards — but advance assurance is strongly recommended because:

  • Investors will ask “do you have advance assurance?” and expect yes
  • If HMRC says no after you have raised, the investors lose their tax relief and you have a very angry cap table
  • The process takes 6-8 weeks, so you need to plan ahead

What you submit with SEIS1:

Document What HMRC wants to see
Business plan What the company does, the product, the market, the business model. Does not need to be a 50-page document — 5-10 pages covering problem, solution, market, revenue model, team, and 2-year roadmap is sufficient
Financial projections 2-3 year profit and loss forecast. Revenue projections, cost base, when you expect to break even. These do not need to be precise — HMRC is checking that the business model is commercial, not that your forecasts are accurate

Where to submit: HMRC’s Small Company Enterprise Centre (SCEC). The form can be submitted online via HMRC’s digital service or by post.

Timeline: HMRC targets 6-8 weeks for a response. In practice it can take longer during busy periods (January-March tax year end is the busiest). Apply at least 10 weeks before you plan to close your round.

Cost: free. No HMRC fee for advance assurance applications.

Response: HMRC will either confirm the company qualifies (advance assurance granted) or raise queries. If queries, you respond and the clock restarts. If refused, you can appeal or restructure and reapply.

Step 3: EIS Advance Assurance (EIS1 form) — apply simultaneously

You can and should apply for both SEIS and EIS advance assurance at the same time. Use form EIS1 for EIS. The information required is nearly identical to SEIS1.

Why apply for both even if you are only raising SEIS now:

  • The EIS assurance is ready when you exhaust your £350k SEIS allowance
  • Some investors may prefer EIS (e.g. if they have already used their SEIS annual allowance elsewhere)
  • It shows investors you have your compliance in order
  • It costs nothing extra

Step 4: Raise and issue shares

Once you have advance assurance, you can raise with confidence. The share issue process:

  1. Agree terms with investors (price per share, number of shares, class of shares)
  2. Adopt bespoke articles if not already done (see articles-guide.md)
  3. Board resolution to allot shares (requires s.551 authority)
  4. Subscription agreements signed by each investor
  5. Shareholders’ agreement signed by all parties
  6. Shares allotted, share certificates issued, register of members updated
  7. File SH01 (return of allotment) at Companies House within one month

Step 5: Compliance Statement (SEIS2/EIS2) — file after shares are issued

After issuing shares to investors, you must file a compliance statement with HMRC:

  • SEIS2 for SEIS shares
  • EIS2 for EIS shares

This confirms that the company still meets the qualifying conditions at the time the shares were actually issued (advance assurance was a forward-looking check; the compliance statement is a backward-looking confirmation).

Timeline: file within 2 years of the end of the tax year in which the shares were issued. But do it immediately — your investors cannot claim their tax relief until you do, and they will chase you.

What you confirm: the company met all qualifying conditions, the shares were issued for genuine new consideration, the money is being used for the qualifying trade.

Step 6: SEIS3/EIS3 certificates — send to investors

After HMRC approves the compliance statement, they generate SEIS3/EIS3 certificates. You send these to each investor. The investor attaches the certificate to their self-assessment tax return to claim the income tax relief.

Timeline: HMRC typically processes compliance statements within 4-6 weeks. During tax year end (January-April), it takes longer.

The investor needs the certificate to claim relief. If you delay filing the compliance statement, the investor cannot claim. Some investors will time their investment to fall in a specific tax year — be aware of the 5 April year-end.

The spending rules — the traps

SEIS: the 70% rule

Before you can raise under EIS, you must have spent at least 70% of the money raised under SEIS on qualifying business activity. This catches people who try to raise SEIS and EIS simultaneously or in quick succession.

Example: you raise £200k under SEIS. Before you can issue EIS shares, you must have spent at least £140k of the SEIS money on the business. If you raise SEIS in January and want to raise EIS in March, you probably have not spent 70% yet — HMRC will refuse the EIS compliance statement.

Planning implication: raise SEIS first, spend it building the product and hiring, then raise EIS when you are ready. Do not try to raise both at the same time. A typical sequence:

Month 0:    SEIS advance assurance granted
Month 1-2:  Raise SEIS round (up to £350k)
Month 2-8:  Build product, hire, spend SEIS money
Month 8:    70% of SEIS money spent
Month 9:    EIS round opens
Month 9-12: Raise EIS round

SEIS: money must not be spent before shares are issued

The SEIS investment must be used for the qualifying trade, but the company must not have spent the specific SEIS money before the shares are actually issued. This sounds contradictory but it means: the investor’s money must arrive and shares must be issued before that money is deployed. In practice:

  • Investor sends money (held in the company’s account)
  • Shares are issued (board resolution, SH01 filed)
  • Then you spend the money

Do not use an investor’s wire transfer to pay a bill before you have formally allotted the shares. If there is a gap — money in but shares not yet issued — keep the money untouched in the account until allotment is complete.

EIS: connected persons

An investor who is “connected” to the company does not qualify for EIS relief. Connected means:

  • An employee or director of the company (with some exceptions — see below)
  • A business partner of a connected person
  • A spouse or civil partner of a connected person
  • A person who holds or is entitled to acquire 30% or more of the company’s share capital

The founder exception for SEIS: unlike EIS, SEIS does allow directors and employees to invest and claim relief, provided they were not connected at the time they subscribed and their directorship/employment began after the share issue or at the same time. In practice, this means a founder who was a director before the SEIS share issue IS connected for SEIS purposes and cannot claim SEIS relief on their own investment.

The practical impact: you (Richard) cannot invest in your own SEIS/EIS round and claim tax relief. The schemes are for external investors only.

EIS: no pre-arranged exit

If there is an arrangement for the investor to sell their shares within 3 years, the relief is withdrawn. This includes buy-back arrangements, put options, or any other mechanism that guarantees the investor can exit. The investment must carry genuine risk.

EIS: share class

SEIS/EIS shares must be ordinary shares (or shares that carry no preferential right to the company’s assets on winding up beyond a right to the return of the amount subscribed). In practice, this means:

  • SEIS/EIS shares can be a separate class of ordinary share with some additional rights (information rights, consent rights)
  • They cannot be preference shares with a liquidation preference
  • The “ordinary shares” in the articles of association are suitable for SEIS/EIS

Important tension: institutional investors want preferred shares with a liquidation preference. Angel investors want SEIS/EIS relief. You cannot have both on the same shares. The solution is typically to issue SEIS/EIS-qualifying ordinary shares to angels at seed, and preferred shares to institutional investors at Series A (by which time you have likely exhausted SEIS and possibly EIS anyway).

Common mistakes

  1. Not applying for advance assurance — raising without it and then finding out you do not qualify. Investors lose tax relief. Relationships damaged.

  2. Missing the 70% spending rule — trying to raise EIS before spending 70% of SEIS money. HMRC refuses the EIS2 compliance statement.

  4. Issuing preference shares — SEIS/EIS shares cannot carry a liquidation preference. Giving angels the same preferred shares as institutional investors disqualifies the tax relief.

Issuing Shares to Early Supporters

How to set up a small share pool and issue shares to advisors, early supporters, and pre-investment contributors. The mechanics, the tax, the traps.

First decision: shares or options?

For early supporters, shares are usually simpler and more appropriate. Options are better for employees and people who will contribute over time. The rest of this guide covers shares.

The steps, in order

Step 1: Adopt bespoke articles FIRST

Do not issue shares to anyone while Model Articles are in force.

Model Articles have almost no transfer restrictions. A supporter could receive shares and immediately transfer them to anyone — a competitor, an ex-partner, a random third party. You would have no right to buy them back and no pre-emption rights.

The bespoke articles (in articles-of-association.md) include: - Pre-emption rights on transfer (articles 25-32) - Drag-along and tag-along (articles 33-34) - Leaver / vesting provisions (articles 35-40) - Board approval of transfers (article 31)

These must be in place before the first share goes to anyone other than you.

Process: pass a special resolution to adopt the bespoke articles, file CC04 at Companies House. You hold 100% of votes, so you pass it alone. Do this before step 2.

Step 2: Decide the pool size

Common pool sizes for early supporters (pre-investment):

Pool size Typical use
1-2% Individual advisor (meets monthly, makes introductions)
0.25-1% Early supporter (gave feedback, opened doors, helped with a specific problem)
5-10% Option pool reserved for future hires (investors will expect this, but it is usually created at investment, not now)
2-5% total Aggregate for all early supporters combined

Keep the total small. Every share you issue now dilutes you. Investors will look at your cap table and ask why 10% is already gone before they arrived. 2-5% total across all early supporters is reasonable. More than that raises questions.

Be specific about what each person contributed. “They helped early on” is vague. “They introduced us to our first three pilot customers” or “they advised on the EAV architecture and attended monthly calls for 6 months” is defensible.

Step 3: Value the shares

The shares have a market value. For tax purposes, HMRC will want to know what that value is. At pre-revenue, just incorporated, no investment, the market value should be very low — potentially just the nominal value (£0.0001 per share if that is your share structure).

How to establish value:

Method When to use
Nominal value Just incorporated, no revenue, no investment, no significant assets. The shares are worth their nominal value (e.g. £0.0001 each). This is defensible if the company genuinely has no value yet.
HMRC valuation (SAV) If you are unsure, you can ask HMRC’s Shares and Assets Valuation team to agree a value. Free, but takes 4-6 weeks. Gives certainty.
Independent valuation If the company has raised money, has revenue, or has significant IP. An accountant or valuer determines the market value.
Last round price If you have already raised investment, the price per share from the last round is a strong indicator of market value.

At your stage (just incorporated, pre-trading, no investment): the shares are worth nominal value or very close to it. The supporter pays nominal value per share (e.g. 10,000 shares x £0.0001 = £1.00). No tax issue because they paid market value.

The danger: if you issue shares to a supporter after the company has built significant product, acquired customers, or raised money, HMRC may argue the market value is higher than nominal. The supporter would owe income tax on the difference between what they paid and the market value. This is why timing matters — issue early, when the value is genuinely low.

Step 4: Section 431 election

When you issue shares that are subject to restrictions (vesting, leaver provisions, transfer restrictions), the shares are technically “restricted securities” under the Income Tax (Earnings and Pensions) Act 2003 (ITEPA), sections 423-432.

Without a s.431 election, HMRC can tax the supporter again when restrictions are lifted (e.g. when shares vest). The s.431 election tells HMRC: “tax me on the unrestricted market value now, and don’t tax me again later.”

In practice: at nominal value, the tax is zero either way. But you should still file the election because: - It creates certainty — no future tax surprises - Investors and their solicitors will ask “were s.431 elections filed?” during due diligence - It costs nothing to file

How: both parties (the company and the recipient) sign the election within 14 days of the share issue. Keep a copy. It is a one-page form.

Step 5: Authority to allot (s.551 Companies Act 2006)

The directors need authority from shareholders to allot new shares. This is granted by ordinary resolution.

As sole shareholder, you pass this resolution yourself. The resolution should: - Authorise the directors to allot shares up to a specified amount (e.g. “up to [X] ordinary shares”) - Specify the period of authority (typically 5 years) - Cover the specific allotment you are about to make

Template resolution:

The sole member of the Company RESOLVES as an ordinary resolution that, pursuant to section 551 of the Companies Act 2006, the directors are generally and unconditionally authorised to allot shares in the Company up to an aggregate nominal amount of £[AMOUNT] for a period of five years from the date of this resolution, such authority being in addition to any existing authority.

Step 6: Disapplication of statutory pre-emption rights (s.561)

Under the Companies Act, when a company issues new shares for cash, it must first offer them to existing shareholders in proportion to their holdings. Since you are the only shareholder, you need to disapply this right so the shares can go directly to the supporter.

This requires a special resolution (75% — which you pass alone as 100% shareholder).

Template resolution:

The sole member of the Company RESOLVES as a special resolution that, pursuant to section 569 of the Companies Act 2006, the directors are empowered to allot equity securities for cash pursuant to the authority granted by [the s.551 resolution above] as if section 561 of the Act did not apply to any such allotment, such power to expire at the end of the period of authority granted under that resolution.

Step 7: Subscriber’s letter / share subscription agreement

A short agreement between the Company and the supporter covering:

Term What to include
Parties The Company and the supporter
Shares Number of shares, class (ordinary), nominal value
Subscription price What the supporter pays per share (usually nominal value at this stage)
Vesting The vesting schedule (see below)
Restrictions Reference to the articles — pre-emption, transfer restrictions, leaver provisions all apply
Deed of adherence The supporter signs the deed of adherence to the articles (Schedule 2 of the bespoke articles)
s.431 election Both parties sign the election (attached as a schedule)
Warranties The supporter warrants they are acquiring the shares for their own account, not as nominee
Confidentiality The terms of the arrangement are confidential

This does not need to be long. 3-5 pages plus the deed of adherence and s.431 election as schedules.

Step 8: Vesting schedule

Shares issued to supporters should vest over time. If a supporter disappears after a month, you do not want them holding unrestricted shares. The vesting schedule and leaver provisions in the bespoke articles (articles 35-40) provide the mechanism.

Typical vesting for advisors/supporters:

Type Schedule
Advisor (ongoing) 24 months, monthly vesting, no cliff. Conditional on continued advisory services.
Early supporter (contribution complete) Immediate vesting on all shares. If their contribution is already done and you are rewarding past work, vesting is not appropriate — they have already earned it.
Hybrid 50% immediate (for past contribution), 50% over 12-24 months (for ongoing involvement).

The leaver provisions in the articles apply automatically. If a supporter with unvested shares stops being involved: - Good Leaver: unvested shares bought back at Fair Market Value - Bad Leaver: unvested shares bought back at nominal value

“Good Leaver” for a supporter would need to be defined in the subscription agreement (since the articles define it in terms of employment/directorship). Typically: a supporter is a Good Leaver if they have fulfilled their advisory obligations and are leaving amicably.

Step 9: Board resolution to allot

Pass a board resolution (you, as sole director) resolving to: - Allot [number] ordinary shares of £[nominal value] each to [supporter name] - At a subscription price of £[price] per share - Subject to the terms of the subscription agreement - Conditional on the supporter signing the deed of adherence and s.431 election

Record this in formal board minutes. Declare your interest under s.177 if the supporter is someone you have a personal relationship with (friend, family).

Step 10: Execution day

On the same day:

  1. Supporter signs the subscription agreement
  2. Supporter signs the deed of adherence (Schedule 2 of the articles)
  3. Both parties sign the s.431 election
  4. Supporter pays the subscription price (even if it is £1 — the payment must actually happen)
  5. Board resolution records the allotment
  6. Update the register of members

Step 11: File at Companies House

Within one month of allotment, file form SH01 (return of allotment of shares) at Companies House. This notifies Companies House that new shares have been issued. Include: - Number of shares allotted - Class of shares - Nominal value - Amount paid (including any premium) - Name and address of the allottee

Step 12: Issue share certificate

Within two months of allotment, issue a share certificate to the supporter. The certificate states: - Company name and number - Name of shareholder - Number and class of shares - Amount paid up - Signed by a director

Step 13: File s.431 election

Keep the signed s.431 election with the company’s records. You do not file it with HMRC proactively — but you must produce it if HMRC asks, and the supporter must reference it on their self-assessment return if they report a chargeable event.

Step 14: Update cap table

Update your cap table spreadsheet to reflect the new shareholder, their shares, their vesting schedule, and the dilution to your holding.

Step 15: Notify your accountant

Tell your accountant that you have issued shares. They may need to: - Report it on the company’s annual return of securities (form 42, filed with HMRC) - Include it in the company’s annual accounts - Advise the supporter on their personal tax position

Tax implications — summary

For the supporter

Scenario Tax consequence
Pays market value for the shares No income tax. No NI. They bought shares at fair price.
Pays less than market value (e.g. nominal value when market value is higher) Income tax on the difference (market value minus price paid), treated as employment income or miscellaneous income. NI may also apply.

At your stage: market value is nominal. Supporter pays nominal value. With s.431 election filed. Tax consequence: zero.

For you (dilution, not tax)

Issuing shares to a supporter is not a taxable event for you. You are diluted (your percentage decreases) but you do not owe tax on that dilution. Dilution is an economic cost, not a tax cost.

For the company

No corporation tax consequence from issuing shares at market value. If you issue shares at below market value to someone who provides services (an advisor), the company may be able to claim a corporation tax deduction for the cost of the services received. Your accountant can advise.

SEIS/EIS implications

Does issuing supporter shares affect SEIS/EIS qualification?

Probably not, but check.

  • Gross assets test (SEIS): must be under £350k. Issuing shares at nominal value adds almost nothing to gross assets.
  • Number of employees: supporters are not employees unless you put them on payroll. Advisory relationships do not count.
  • Connected persons: a supporter who holds 30%+ of the company’s shares after issue would be a “connected person” and could not claim SEIS/EIS relief on their own investment. At 1-2% this is not an issue.
  • Share class: supporter shares should be the same class of ordinary shares that SEIS/EIS investors will receive (or a separate class, but not preferred). Keep it simple — issue the same ordinary shares.
  • Prior investment: issuing shares to supporters is not “investment under EIS or VCT” — it does not trigger the “no prior qualifying investment” test for SEIS.

The main risk: if a supporter is also a potential investor, they are now a shareholder. Their SEIS/EIS eligibility depends on their percentage holding (must be under 30%) and whether they are an employee or director (connected person test). Check each supporter individually.

Common mistakes

  1. Issuing shares before adopting bespoke articles. The supporter now holds shares with no transfer restrictions, no pre-emption rights, and no leaver provisions. Very hard to fix retroactively.

  2. Not filing the s.431 election within 14 days. The window is strict. If you miss it, the supporter faces potential double taxation on restricted shares.

  3. Not filing SH01 within one month. Late filing is a criminal offence (though rarely prosecuted for short delays). File promptly.

  4. Giving too much equity. 5% to an advisor who “promised to help” but has no binding commitment. When they disappear, you have a 5% shareholder you cannot get rid of. Keep individual allocations small (0.5-2%) and attach vesting.

  5. No vesting on shares for ongoing involvement. If the deal is “advise us for 2 years,” the shares should vest over 2 years. If the advisor stops after 3 months, you can buy back the unvested shares.

  6. Verbal agreements. “I’ll give you some shares” with no written terms. Six months later, you disagree on how many, on what terms, subject to what conditions. Get it in writing before issuing.

  7. Not considering the investor’s perspective. Before your first round, an investor will look at the cap table and ask about every shareholder. “Who is Advisor A? What do they do? Why do they have 3%?” Have clear answers.

  8. Forgetting to tell your accountant. The company has a reporting obligation (form 42) for share issues to directors and employees. Even if the supporter is neither, tell your accountant so they can advise on reporting.

Naming conventions

Consistent naming makes documents findable years later. Follow these rules:

Files

[Type] - [Description] - [Version or Date].ext

Examples: - IP Assignment Deed - Richard Targett - 2026-02-14.pdf - Service Agreement - Richard Targett - 2026-02-14.pdf - NDA - Acme Ventures - 2026-03-15.pdf - Board Minutes - 001 - IP Assignment & Service Agreement - 2026-02-14.pdf - Financial Model v3 - 2026-06-01.xlsx - Pitch Deck v2 - 2026-06-01.pdf

Dates

Always use YYYY-MM-DD format. It sorts chronologically in any file system.

  • 2026-02-14 not 14/02/2026 or Feb 14 2026
  • In folder names for monthly documents: 2026-02 February (so they sort by date but are still readable)

Versions

For documents that evolve (pitch deck, financial model, business plan):

  • v1, v2, v3 — major revisions
  • Keep old versions in an Archive/ subfolder within the same directory
  • The current version is always in the main folder, prefixed with [CURRENT]

For documents that are executed once and never change (signed deeds, certificates):

  • Use the date of execution instead of a version number
  • These never go into Archive — they are permanent records

The [CURRENT] prefix

The single most useful convention. Any folder with evolving documents uses [CURRENT] to mark the live version:

Articles/
├── [CURRENT] Model Articles.pdf           ← this is what's in force right now
└── [templates]/
    └── Bespoke Articles v1 (draft).pdf    ← this is a draft for future use

After adopting bespoke articles:

Articles/
├── [CURRENT] Bespoke Articles - Adopted 2026-09-15.pdf
└── Archive/
    └── Model Articles (replaced 2026-09-15).pdf

One glance tells you what is current. No ambiguity.

Signed deeds, certificates, formation documents — these never change. They are historical records. File them with their date and leave them.

The execution workflow

When you execute a document (e.g. the IP Assignment Deed):

Common mistakes

  1. Dumping everything in one folder. Three years later you have 200 files and cannot find anything. The folder structure is the index.

  2. Not scanning signed documents. The paper original matters, but if your house floods, the paper is gone. Scan everything to Drive the same day you sign it. The scan is the backup.

  3. Using personal accounts. If documents live in your personal Google account and you lose access to it, the company loses its records. Use a company Google Workspace account.

  4. Not archiving superseded versions. When articles change, the old version disappears into a renamed file or gets deleted. Always move the old version to Archive with a note of when and why it was replaced. The history is valuable.

  5. Giving blanket edit access. Your accountant needs to edit the annual accounts, not your entire finance folder. Share at the most specific level possible.

  6. Forgetting to revoke data room access. After a fundraise, revoke the investor’s access to the data room folder. They have their own copies of the documents they signed. They do not need ongoing access to your full due diligence pack.

  7. Not keeping the git templates in sync. If a solicitor modifies your articles template during a funding round, update the git version too. The git repo should always reflect the latest template, even if the signed version lives in Drive.

  8. Storing secrets in Google Drive. Passwords, API keys, encryption keys — these do not belong in Drive. Use a password manager (1Password, Bitwarden) for credentials and agenix for infrastructure secrets.

Document Brand System — Google Workspace Templates

How to set up Google Docs, Sheets, and Slides so every company document looks like it came from the same company. Do this once, use it forever.

The problem

Without templates, every document looks different. Board minutes in Times New Roman, pitch decks in whatever Google’s default is, financial models in Arial. Investors notice. It signals “this person doesn’t pay attention to detail” — which is the opposite of what you want.

Workflow

  1. Need a new document? Go to _Templates/
  2. Right-click the appropriate template > Make a copy
  3. Rename the copy and move it to the correct folder
  4. Start writing — fonts, colours, headers, and layout are already set

Rules

  • Never edit the templates directly. Always make a copy. If you want to improve a template, make the change in the template file and save it — but never use the template file as a working document.
  • Every external-facing document uses a template. Board minutes, agreements, pitch decks, letters, financial models. No exceptions.
  • Internal notes and scratch docs can be plain. Not everything needs to be branded. Quick notes, internal checklists, brainstorm docs — use Google’s default. Save the branding effort for documents that leave the building.
  • PDF exports for signing. When a document needs to be signed (agreements, deeds), export as PDF from Google Docs. The formatting carries over cleanly. Sign the PDF, not the Google Doc.

What this signals to investors

When an investor receives your pitch deck, board minutes, financial model, and legal documents, and they all use the same fonts, the same colours, the same layout conventions — it signals:

  • Attention to detail
  • Organisational discipline
  • A founder who thinks about how things look and feel (relevant for a product company)
  • Professionalism beyond what is typical for a pre-seed company

It takes 30 minutes to set up. It pays dividends for years.

Minutes of a Meeting of the Board of Directors

FORMABI LTD (the “Company”)

Company Number: [COMPANY NUMBER]

Date: [DATE]

Time: [TIME]

Place: [ADDRESS or “Held remotely by electronic means”]

Present: Richard Targett (sole director and Chair)

In attendance: None

1. Chair and quorum

Richard Targett acted as Chair of the meeting. The Chair noted that as the Company currently has only one director, the quorum for a board meeting is one in accordance with Article 14(2) of the Company’s Articles of Association. The meeting was accordingly quorate.

2. Purpose of meeting

The Chair stated that the purpose of the meeting was to consider and approve:

  1. a Deed of Assignment of Intellectual Property Rights between Richard Targett and the Company; and

  2. a Service Agreement between the Company and Richard Targett.

3. Declaration of interest

The Chair declared, pursuant to section 177 of the Companies Act 2006, that he has a direct personal interest in the matters to be discussed at this meeting, being:

  1. the proposed assignor under the IP Assignment Deed; and

  2. the proposed employee under the Service Agreement.

The Chair noted that as the sole director of the Company, there are no other directors to whom this declaration can be made in the usual manner. This declaration is recorded in these minutes as a matter of proper corporate governance and to comply with the spirit of section 177.

The Chair further noted that, as the sole member of the Company, he also consents in his capacity as shareholder to the Company entering into these transactions, in accordance with sections 175-177 and 180 of the Act.

4. IP Assignment Deed

The Chair presented the proposed Deed of Assignment of Intellectual Property Rights, under which Richard Targett would assign to the Company all intellectual property rights created by him prior to and since the incorporation of the Company, to the extent relating to the Company’s business.

The Chair noted the following:

  1. the assigned intellectual property includes the Formabi application source code, the company-as-code system, all brand assets, domain names, and related materials;

  2. the consideration for the assignment is £1.00 and the ordinary shares already held by the Assignor;

  3. the assignment is necessary to ensure that the Company is the legal and beneficial owner of all intellectual property used in its business — without it, the intellectual property would remain the personal property of Richard Targett and the Company would have no assets of substance;

  4. the terms of the Deed are on arm’s length terms and are in the best interests of the Company.

IT WAS RESOLVED that the Company enter into the IP Assignment Deed in the form presented to the meeting, and that Richard Targett be authorised to execute the Deed on behalf of the Company.

5. Service Agreement

The Chair presented the proposed Service Agreement between the Company and Richard Targett, under which Richard Targett would be employed as Chief Executive Officer.

The Chair noted the following key terms and the rationale for each:

Salary: £0 per annum

The Company is pre-revenue and has limited cash resources. A salary of £0 is appropriate at this stage and is consistent with the position of a sole founder who is funding the Company through their own time and effort. The salary is to be reviewed by the Board following the Company’s first funding round. This approach preserves cash for product development and is standard practice for pre-revenue founder-led companies.

Notice period: 3 months (mutual)

A 3-month notice period balances the Company’s need for continuity with the Executive’s flexibility. This is within the range that investors at seed stage would expect (typically 3-6 months). A longer period would be disproportionate at this stage.

Post-termination restrictions: 6 months

The non-compete, non-solicit, and non-dealing restrictions are each limited to 6 months. This period is reasonable and proportionate given:

  • the early stage of the Company;
  • the limited geographic scope (the Company operates primarily in the UK and online);
  • the need to protect the Company’s business, customer relationships, and confidential information;
  • the requirement under English law that restrictive covenants go no further than reasonably necessary to protect a legitimate business interest.

A restriction of 6 months is at the lower end of the typical range (6-12 months) and is therefore more likely to be enforceable.

IP assignment

The Service Agreement includes a comprehensive intellectual property assignment clause covering all works created during employment. This complements the IP Assignment Deed (which covers pre-existing IP) and ensures that all intellectual property — past and future — belongs to the Company.

Working Time Regulations opt-out

The Executive has opted out of the 48-hour weekly working time limit under Regulation 4 of the Working Time Regulations 1998. This is standard for senior executives and founders.

Overall assessment

The Chair confirmed that:

  1. the terms of the Service Agreement are reasonable, on arm’s length terms, and in the best interests of the Company;

  2. the terms are consistent with market practice for sole-founder pre-revenue companies;

  3. the Service Agreement is being entered into to formalise the Executive’s relationship with the Company, to ensure proper IP assignment on an ongoing basis, and to establish a governance framework that will satisfy investor due diligence requirements;

  4. no alternative terms were considered that would be more favourable to the Company, given that the Executive’s salary is £0 and the restrictive covenants are within the standard range.

IT WAS RESOLVED that:

  1. the Company enter into the Service Agreement in the form presented to the meeting;

  2. Richard Targett be authorised to execute the Service Agreement on behalf of the Company;

  3. the Service Agreement take effect from the Commencement Date specified therein.

6. Conflict of interest — acknowledgement

The Chair acknowledged that he has approved transactions in which he has a direct personal interest. The Chair recorded the following for the avoidance of doubt:

  1. as sole director, there is no mechanism for the interested director to recuse themselves from the decision — the Act permits a sole director to make decisions notwithstanding their interest;

  2. as sole shareholder, Richard Targett consents to the transactions and waives any right to challenge them on grounds of conflict of interest;

  3. the terms of both the IP Assignment Deed and the Service Agreement have been set at arm’s length and on terms that are reasonable and in the best interests of the Company;

  4. these minutes constitute a full and transparent record of the decisions made, the interests declared, and the rationale for the terms approved, so that any future director, shareholder, or investor can understand the basis on which these arrangements were entered into.

7. Close

There being no further business, the meeting was closed.
